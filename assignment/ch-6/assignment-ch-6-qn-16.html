Explanation:
The Capital Asset Pricing Model relies on only one explanatory factor for a securitys (or portfolios) required return and imposes a large number of restrictive and unrealistic assumptions.
The Fama-French three-factor model argues that a securitys required return is a function of the market risk premium, the companys size, and the companys book-to-market value ratio.
The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), developed and presented by William Sharpe in 1964, argues that the required return on an asset or portfolio is affected by the assets sensitivity to the markets systematic risk, the expected return of the market, and the expected return on a risk-free asset. The model is written as follows:
riri =  = rRF + (rM  rRF)birRF + (rM  rRF)bi
The model makes many restrictive assumptions, such as that there is an absence of taxes and transactions costs; that there is perfect divisibility and liquidity of all assets; that the market portfolio, which includes all types of assets that are held by anyone as an investment, is held by every market participant; and that investors consider all their assets and attempt to optimize one portfolio.
The Fama-French three-factor model, developed and presented by Eugene Fama and Kenneth French in 1992 and 1993, is based on the observation that two classes of companies tend to outperform the market: those exhibiting relatively small market values (or levels of capitalization) and those with high book-to-market value ratios. Using 25 portfolios, they then added these two factors to the CAPMs market risk premium to determine a portfolios required return. The model is written as follows:
(ri,t  rRF,t)(ri,t  rRF,t) =  = ai + bi(rM,t  rRF,t) + ci(rSMB,t) + di(rHML,t) + ei,tai + bi(rM,t  rRF,t) + ci(rSMB,t) + di(rHML,t) + ei,t
Here, riri is the historical (realized) return on Stock i; aii is the vertical axis intercept term for Stock i; bii, cii, and dii are slope coefficients for Stock i; rMrM is the historical (realized) return on the market; rRFrRF is the historical (realized) return on the risk-free asset; rSMBrSMB is the historical (realized) return on the small-size portfolio minus the big-size portfolio; rHMLrHML is the historical (realized) return on the high book-to-market value portfolio minus the low book-to-market value portfolio; and eii is the random error term, which represents the difference between the actual return on Stock i in a given period and the return predicted by the regression line. The model has been used more by academic researchers than managers of actual companies due to (1) the lack of usable data, (2) the lack of knowledge of the current or future average returns for the relevant factors, or (3) the fact that the historical return values are stable over time.
Several subsequent studies have shown that this model is not correct by arguing that (1) the size effect never did or no longer influences stock returns, and (2) the book-to-market value effect either is not as significant as first supposed or is not a function of risk.
