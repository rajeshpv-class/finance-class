Business Transaction
Edinburgh Exports Inc. (EEI) sells 25,000 shares of new common stock ($1 per share par value) to new and existing shareholders for $20 per share.
This transaction affects at least three balance sheet accounts. Specifically, it:
Increases EEIs Cash account balance from $15,000 to $515,000
Increases the companys Common Stock account balance, as reported in the equity section of the balance sheet, from $150,000 to $175,000 ($1 per share par value x 25,000 shares)
Increases EEIs Capital Paid-in Excess of Par Value account by $475,000 ($19 per share x 25,000 shares), from $350,000 to $825,000
In this transaction, the asset section of the balance sheet increases due to the increase in the Cash account, whereas the total of the equity section of the balance sheet increases as a result of increases in two equity accounts. Though each new share is sold for $20 per share, the total $20 of cash received is allocated between two equity accounts: The $1 par value per share is recorded in the Common Stock account, and the difference between the sale price of the shares ($19) and the par value ($1) is recorded in the Capital Paid-in Excess of Par account.
This transaction has the potential to affect each of the financial statement accounts that is based on one or more of the Cash, Common Stock, and Capital Paid-in Excess of Par accounts. Therefore, it has the potential to affect ratios that are based on EEIs Current Assets, Total Assets, Total Equity, and Total Liabilities and Equity accounts. For example, the quick ratio increases as a result of this transaction, whereas the total assets turnover ratio and return on assets ratios decrease.
This transaction will increase EEIs current ratio, but decrease the debt and basic earnings power ratios. Here are some examples:
The current ratio increases from 5.00 to 14.95 because the Cash account and Current Asset account balances increase by $500,000, while the Current Liabilities account balance remains unchanged.
Because the Total Assets account increases by $500,000 ($20 per share x 25,000 shares) and the Total Liabilities remain unchanged, the debt ratio will decrease from 27.50% to 15.71%.
The basic earnings power ratio also decreases from 112.50 to 90.00 as the EBIT (the numerator) remains unchanged, but the denominator (total assets) increases from $2 million to $2.50 million.
On the other hand, this transaction does not affect the inventory ratio or the operating profit margin. This transaction also does not affect the accounts used to compute these ratiosInventory, Sales, Net Fixed Assets, and EBIT.
Business Transaction
A $500,000 10-year bank loan is initiated, and the funds are placed in Edinburgh Exports Inc. (EEI)s checking account.
This transaction affects at least two balance sheet accounts. Specifically, it:
Increases EEIs Cash account balance, from $15,000 to $515,000
Increases the companys long-term debt from $500,000 to $1,000,000
This transaction has the potential to affect each of the financial statement accounts based on one or more of these two accounts, including Current Assets, Total Assets, and Total Liabilities balances. Some examples follow:
The increase in the amount of long-term and total debt outstanding will increase the companys debt ratio from 27.50% to 42.00%.
The return on assets will decrease from 76.93% to 61.54% due to an increase in the denominator (total assets), but the numerator (net income) has no change.
The current ratio will increase from 5 to 10 due to the increase in the Cash account and Current Assets account balances, but the Current Liabilities balance has no change.
Another ratio that will decrease as a result of this transaction is the basic earnings power ratios.
On the other hand, this transaction does not affect the inventory and fixed assets turnover ratios, gross profit margin, and operating profit margin.
